# Knowledge Bank — iFAST Roadside (Ontario / East GTA)

**Purpose:** Internal, fact-checked reference for content writers. This file is NOT shipped to the website. Every numeric or legal claim used in published copy must trace back to an entry here. Where Confidence is **Unverified** or **Medium**, writers must hedge the language ("manufacturers commonly state…", "often around…") rather than asserting a hard figure, and should not cite a source that does not actually contain the number.

Context: Ontario, Canada. Service area: Pickering, Ajax, Whitby, Oshawa (Durham Region) and Scarborough (City of Toronto). All entries checked 2026-06.

## TIRES

### Donut / temporary spare — speed and distance limits
- **Claim:** Compact/temporary ("donut") spares are typically limited to a maximum of about 80 km/h (50 mph) and short distance — commonly cited as roughly 50–110 km (about 30–70 miles), with the lower end advised at higher speed, on rough roads, or when towing.
- **Detail:** The exact limit is printed on the spare's sidewall and in the owner's manual and varies by automaker, so writers should tell drivers to check those first. Treat the donut as "get to the nearest service," not a normal tire. On AWD/4WD, change off the donut promptly to avoid drivetrain strain.
- **Source:** https://www.utires.com/articles/how-fast-can-you-drive-on-a-donut-tips-for-safe-driving/ and https://www.autozone.com/diy/trustworthy-advice/how-long-can-i-drive-on-a-spare-tire (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** Medium — 80 km/h is near-universal; the distance figure varies by source and automaker, so phrase as a range / "check your manual."

### Tire repair standard — tread (crown) only, never sidewall, patch-plug combination
- **Claim:** Per U.S. Tire Manufacturers Association (USTMA) guidelines, repairs are limited to the tread (crown) area only — punctures in the sidewall or shoulder/belt-edge area are NOT repairable. The injury must be no greater than 1/4 inch (6 mm) in diameter, the tire must be removed from the rim for full inspection, and a proper permanent repair uses a combination unit: a rubber stem/plug to fill the puncture plus a patch to seal the innerliner. A plug alone is not an acceptable repair.
- **Detail:** This is the industry-recognized standard (USTMA; same substance carried in tire-maker bulletins such as Cooper/Goodyear/Continental). Writers can state plainly that sidewall punctures mean replacement, and that a "string plug" done without demounting is not a permanent fix.
- **Source:** https://www.ustires.org/tire-care-safety/tire-repair-basics (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High

### Wheel re-torque after installation
- **Claim:** After a wheel is installed, lug nuts should be re-torqued after a short break-in distance. Tire manufacturers/industry guidance commonly state ~50 km (Continental); many Canadian tire shops advise checking again at 50–100 km.
- **Detail:** Early driving causes the wheel assembly to settle (thermal cycling, metal seating), which can reduce clamping force, so a re-check restores correct torque. Continental's published guidance is "approximately 50 kilometers"; "100 km" is a common shop convention. Writers should say "after the first 50–100 km" to stay safely within sourced ground.
- **Source:** https://www.continental-tires.com/tire-knowledge/retorquing-wheels/ (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High for the 50 km manufacturer figure; Medium for the "100 km" shop convention (no single authoritative TRAC/TRAC-Canada document located — phrase as a 50–100 km range).

## BATTERY / STARTING SYSTEM

### Battery cranking power loss in cold
- **Claim:** A car battery loses a large share of its cranking power in the cold. Commonly cited figures: at about 0°C (freezing) a battery delivers only ~65% of rated cranking power (i.e., loses roughly a third / "up to ~35%"), and at about −18°C (0°F) it can drop to ~50% of rated power. At the same time the engine needs ~30% more energy to crank when cold.
- **Detail:** Cold slows the battery's chemical reactions while thickening engine oil, so available power falls just as demand rises — which is why weak batteries fail on the first cold morning. The CCA (cold cranking amps) rating is itself defined by Battery Council International testing at −18°C (0°F). Use "around freezing" / "around −18°C" framing.
- **Source:** https://www.autoblog.com/features/is-your-battery-ready-for-freezing-temperatures-how-to-tell-before-it-strands-you and (CCA test temperature −18°C / 0°F) https://www.interstatebatteries.com/blog/understanding-cca-in-car-batteries (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** Medium — the −18°C CCA test temperature is firmly established (BCI standard); the specific 35%/50% loss figures are widely repeated across battery vendors but were not pinned to a single BCI/CAA primary document. Phrase as "loses up to about a third near freezing and roughly half near −18°C," not as an exact law.

### Battery vs alternator vs starter — distinguishing symptoms
- **Claim:** Different failures give different symptoms. Dead/weak BATTERY: nothing or only rapid clicking when you turn the key, dim dash/headlights while cranking, may start after a boost. STARTER: a single loud click (or silence) with dash lights staying normal, engine won't crank. ALTERNATOR: car starts (especially after a jump) but then stalls/dies, and/or lights dim or flicker while driving as it fails to recharge the battery.
- **Detail:** Quick rule of thumb writers can use: clicking/no-crank with dim lights → battery; one solid click with normal lights and no crank → starter; runs then dies / dimming while driving → alternator. Symptoms can overlap (a bad battery overworks the alternator/starter), so recommend a proper test.
- **Source:** https://www.autozone.com/diy/alternator/alternator-vs-starter (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High

## FUEL SYSTEM

### Running on empty shortens in-tank fuel pump life
- **Claim:** Chronically driving with very low fuel can shorten the life of an in-tank electric fuel pump, because the surrounding fuel cools and lubricates the pump.
- **Detail:** When the tank runs low the pump is no longer fully submerged, loses its cooling/lubrication, runs hotter and works harder to draw fuel — accelerating wear; running completely dry can cause sudden failure. Practical advice: keep at least a quarter tank.
- **Source:** https://www.cbsnews.com/boston/news/running-on-empty-can-damage-fuel-pumps/ and https://gmb.net/blog/maximize-fuel-pump-lifespan/ (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High (mechanism); Medium on any precise "X% shorter life" figure — none is well-sourced, so avoid quoting a number.

### Misfuel (wrong fuel in tank) — correct immediate action
- **Claim:** If you put the wrong fuel in your vehicle (diesel in a gas car, or gasoline in a diesel), do NOT start the engine and do not turn the ignition on — have the vehicle towed and the tank professionally drained/flushed.
- **Detail:** Starting (or even keying on) the vehicle pulls the wrong fuel into the fuel system and engine, turning a drain-and-flush job into major repairs (gas in a diesel is especially damaging because diesel lubricates the fuel system and gas ignites prematurely). Leave the key out and call for a tow/drain service.
- **Source:** https://www.statefarm.com/simple-insights/auto-and-vehicles/what-to-do-if-you-put-the-wrong-fuel-in-a-car (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High

## TOWING

### Flatbed vs wheel-lift for AWD / 4WD / EV
- **Claim:** AWD, 4WD and many EVs should be towed on a flatbed (all four wheels off the ground). Towing with the driven wheels still on the road can damage the transmission, transfer case or differentials — and on EVs can damage the electric drive motor or trigger faults — because the wheels spin the driveline while the engine is off and fluid isn't circulating to lubricate/cool it.
- **Detail:** Automatic transmissions especially need the engine running to circulate fluid, so towing with drive wheels down risks overheating/seizing. For AWD a flatbed is the safe default; for EVs, towing on the drive wheels can cause permanent damage. Always confirm with the owner's manual, but for roadside copy: "AWD/4WD/EV → flatbed."
- **Source:** https://www.goodhandstowing.com/do-not-tow-that-4x4-or-awd-until-you-read-this/ (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High (general principle; exact safe-tow method is model-specific — direct readers to their manual / a flatbed).

### Ontario towing consumer rights — TSSEA, 2021
- **Claim:** Under Ontario's Towing and Storage Safety and Enforcement Act, 2021 (in force Jan 1, 2024), consumers have the right to: (1) give consent before their vehicle is towed (unless police/authorized official directs it, e.g. illegal parking); (2) be told the rates for towing/storage before service; (3) choose where the vehicle is towed (except in restricted tow zones); (4) receive an itemized invoice showing each service and its cost before paying; (5) access the storage facility free of charge during operating hours to retrieve belongings; and (6) choose payment method (credit, debit or cash).
- **Detail:** Operators and drivers must hold provincial certificates and must not charge without documented consent. This is the official ontario.ca consumer guidance — safe to cite directly in copy aimed at stranded drivers.
- **Source:** https://www.ontario.ca/page/know-your-rights-when-getting-tow (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High

## PEDESTRIAN SAFETY (LEGAL)

### Walking on a 400-series / controlled-access highway is prohibited
- **Claim:** In Ontario it is illegal for pedestrians to walk on the parts of controlled-access highways listed in the regulation (this covers the 400-series freeways and their on/off ramps), under R.R.O. 1990, Reg. 627 (Use of Controlled-Access Highways by Pedestrians) made under the Highway Traffic Act.
- **Detail:** The regulation prohibits pedestrians on the scheduled controlled-access highways, with limited exceptions including where use is necessary because of an emergency, and crossing at a traffic signal or crosswalk. Practical roadside message: if stranded on a 400-series highway, stay with the vehicle / get behind the barrier where safe and call for help — do not walk along the freeway.
- **Source:** https://www.ontario.ca/laws/regulation/R16025 (e-Laws, "Use of Controlled-Access Highways by Pedestrians") and https://www.canlii.org/en/on/laws/regu/rro-1990-reg-627/latest/rro-1990-reg-627.html (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High (prohibition + emergency exception confirmed). Note: the e-Laws page renders dynamically; CanLII mirrors the same RRO 1990 Reg. 627 text and is the readable confirmation.

## POTHOLE / ROAD-DAMAGE CLAIMS

### City of Toronto pothole damage claim (covers Scarborough)
- **Claim:** To claim vehicle damage caused by a road condition (pothole) against the City of Toronto, you must submit notice of the claim within 10 days of the incident, using the City's Claim Submission Web Form. Reporting a pothole to 311 is NOT the same as filing a claim.
- **Detail:** The City investigates whether it met the provincial Minimum Maintenance Standards (e.g., repair-time windows by road class); if it did, it is generally not liable. Keep evidence: date/time/exact location, photos of the pothole and damage, and repair invoices/estimates. Claims inquiry line: 416-397-4212. Scarborough is part of the City of Toronto, so Scarborough road-damage claims go to the City of Toronto.
- **Source:** https://www.toronto.ca/city-government/accountability-operations-customer-service/complaints-claims-compliments/make-a-claim-against-the-city/pothole-claims/ (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** High (10-day notice and web-form process confirmed on the City page).

### Durham Region + local municipalities pothole/road-damage claims (Pickering, Ajax, Whitby, Oshawa)
- **Claim:** In Durham, roads are maintained by different authorities — the Province, the Regional Municipality of Durham (regional roads), and the local area municipality (local town/city roads). You file against whichever authority maintains the road where the damage occurred. Claims against the Region of Durham are filed using the Region's online Municipal Claim Form; claims for local roads go to the relevant Town/City (Pickering, Ajax, Whitby, Oshawa).
- **Detail:** Durham's claims page does not publish a specific notice-period number and instead warns that "limitation periods and other procedural timelines may apply" and that the Region only considers a claim if it is legally responsible. Drivers should (a) identify whether the road is a regional or local road, (b) consult their own insurer first, and (c) file promptly — do not assume the deadline is long. Evidence needed mirrors Toronto: location, date, photos, and repair estimates.
- **Source:** https://www.durham.ca/en/regional-government/insurance-claims.aspx and https://www.durham.ca/en/regional-government/pothole-issue.aspx (checked 2026-06)
- **Confidence:** Medium — the Region/local split and the Municipal Claim Form are confirmed; the exact notice deadline for the Region and for each local municipality is NOT published on the Region page (writers must NOT state a specific Durham/local deadline — say "file promptly; deadlines may be short, confirm with the municipality").

### Per-city pothole claim authority — quick table
| City / area | Road type | File claim with |
|---|---|---|
| Scarborough | All Scarborough roads | City of Toronto (10-day notice, web form) |
| Pickering | Regional road | Region of Durham (Municipal Claim Form) |
| Pickering | Local road | City of Pickering |
| Ajax | Regional road | Region of Durham (Municipal Claim Form) |
| Ajax | Local road | Town of Ajax |
| Whitby | Regional road | Region of Durham (Municipal Claim Form) |
| Whitby | Local road | Town of Whitby |
| Oshawa | Regional road | Region of Durham (Municipal Claim Form) |
| Oshawa | Local road | City of Oshawa |
| Any 400-series / provincial highway (e.g. 401, 407, 412, 418) | Provincial highway | Province of Ontario (MTO) — not the city/region |

When in doubt, the driver should confirm with the municipality before filing, because filing with the wrong authority wastes the (possibly short) notice window.
